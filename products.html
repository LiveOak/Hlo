Consulting for Statistical Analysis and Software Development

Most services and software produced by Howard Live Oak have been develop for private clients. Discussing these products could reveal proprietary information, or provide insight into systems managing protected health information (PHI). However the following products are available to the public.

NlsyLinks

 

NlsyLinks is an R package developed for the NIH grant "NLSY Kinship Links: Reliable and Valid Sibling Identification" (Joe Rodgers, PI). It provides utilities and kinship information for Behavior Genetics and Developmental research using the NLSY (National Longitudinal Survey of Youth). The release version and documentation is available on CRAN. The development version is available on R-Forge.

The kinships were inferred with code written in C# and R, which is available on the NlsyLinksDetermination repository on GitHub. A list of articles and vignettes involving the NLSY79 and NLSY79-C kinship links can be found the repository's wiki. Team members include Joe Rodgers (Vanderbilt, Psychology; Univeristy of Oklahoma, Psychology), David Bard (University of Oklahoma Health Sciences Center, Pediatrics), Kelly Meredith (University of Oklahoma, Psychology), and Mike Hunter (University of Oklahoma, Psychology).

BBAID: The Balance Beam Aid for Instruction in Diagnosis

 

BBAID is a product developed with Rob Hamm, PhD, in the Family and Preventive Medicine department in the University of Oklahoma Health Sciences Center. It graphically demonstrates a simplified process of differential diagnosis for teaching purposes. Each diagnosis is represented by a side of a balance beam. As a clinician specifies the evidence (e.g., lab tests or patient symptoms), the diagram places a token on the beam to indicate the new center of gravity. Two journal articles are in their second stage of the submission process. If you're interested in the manuscripts, please contact Rob.

Currently BBAID is implemented as a Windows Presentation Foundation (WPF) .NET GUI, deployed with ClickOnce technology; WPF was chosen because rendering tasks are offloaded to the GPU, which permits the balance beam elements to be quickly redrawn as a user hovers over different symptoms in the right panel. Only Windows is currently supported, but we hope to receive additional grant funding to bring it on other platforms. The primary download location is hosted by Family Medicine; in case that is temporarily unavailable, please use this backup location.

EMOSA of Religiousity

 

Andrey Koval is interested in dynamic models of religiosity, history and philosophy of science, and the visualization methods of statistical models through interactive web applications. A recent project tapped into most of those interests. He applied EMOSA (Epidemic Models of the Onset of Social Activities) to the religiosity of NLSY97 participants as they transitioned from they mid-teens to mid-twenties, and visualized the trends for each age cohort.

Andrey invited me to assist the modeling effort. The longitudinal trends are modeled with Bayesian statistics and estimated with Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) using jags and R. If you’re interested in the project, please contact Andrey or visit the project’s GitHub repository, or his LinkedIn and ResearchGate pages.

Univariate Sampling Bootstrap

 

The univariate sampling bootstrap is a statistical technique described in the 2007 Psychological Methods article, "Bootstrapping to test for nonzero population correlation coefficients using univariate sampling".  The article was accompanied with software with a GUI, written in C#. The program can be downloaded here and instructions can be found here.
