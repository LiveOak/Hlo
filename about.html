Will Beasley, President

 

I started Howard Live Oak in 2003 to facilitate my interest in statistical analysis and research methodology. After graduating Davidson College in 2000 with a major in Psychology and a concentration in Neuroscience, I became increasingly interested in research; meanwhile I was involved in several studies with interesting software development challenges.  Almost all my projects since have involved a blend of statistics and software.  I graduated from the Quantitative Psychology program at the University of Oklahoma, Norman in 2010 and subsequently accepted an assistant faculty position athe University of Oklahoma Health Sciences Center in Oklahoma City.

I embrace both Bayesian and Frequentist statistics –a great deal of past work focused on Bayesian methods to estimate models in environmental microbiology, medical decision making, cognitive psychology, and epidemic transmission of social activities. I’ve written several articles and chapters on simulation methods (MCMCs and bootstrapping). Perhaps my biggest strength is exploratory and graphical data analysis. I have compiled a collection of my graphs to use when I teach graphing workshops that I'll be happy to distribute by email upon request.

My programming abilities are as strong as most professional software developers. I’ve developed: (a) four clinical databases with a SQL Server backend and C# middle and client tiers, (b) massive parallel statistical simulations, (c) medical decision making training aids, and (d) at least a dozen systems that manipulate and merge multiple data sources to produce real-time graphics and reporting. For these types of projects, I use many agile and TDD approaches. Regarding Linux, I use OU’s supercomputer (which runs Red Hat) for spatial analyses.

My preferred statistical tool is R, and I have used it extensively since 2006 (I have also taught courses and workshops with SAS and SPSS). I’ve developed one R package (NlsyLinks) and have taught many programming and graphing workshops in R. The appeal of literate programming and reproducible research prompted me to manage my R projects in GitHub and R-Forge and to use knitr/Sweave/ LaTeX for reports. R’s reporting ecosystem has allowed me to communicate better with a new audience (specifically, policy makers and politicians in the past two years), who have relatively limited statistical training.

I am an Assistant Professor of Research at the University of Oklahoma Health Sciences Center, and typically have an additional two or three consulting projects. The time required by my faculty position depends on my outside projects and varies between .5 and .8 full-time employment (I am fortunate to have standing offers from two research studies to hire me for as many hours as I can give them). I prefer to limit my outside projects because I give extraordinary attention to each one, and they expose new challenges. I started a (one-person) consulting company before graduate school, and have had contracts for 10-20 hours per week since then. If you’d like more background, feel free to ask.

A list of my publications can be found at Google Scholar. Additional information is available on LinkedIn and ResearchGate.
