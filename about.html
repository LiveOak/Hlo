Will Beasley, President

 

I started Howard Live Oak in 2003 to facilitate my interests in statistical analysis and research methodology. After graduating Davidson College in 2000 with a Psychology major and a Neuroscience concentration, I became increasingly attracted to research; meanwhile I was involved in several studies with interesting software development challenges. We have had continuous funding and contracts since our company was founded, and almost all have involved a blend of statistics and software.

I attended the Quantitative Psychology graduate program at the University of Oklahoma, Norman (2003-2010) and subsequently accepted an assistant research faculty position at the University of Oklahoma Health Sciences Center in Oklahoma City. The time required by my faculty position depends on my Howard Live Oak projects and varies between .5 and .8 full-time employment (I am fortunate to have standing offers from two research studies to hire me for as many hours as I can give them). I prefer to limit my outside projects because they expose new challenges, and I give extraordinary attention to each one. If you’d like more background, feel free to ask.

I embrace both Bayesian and Frequentist statistics –a great deal of past work focused on Bayesian methods to estimate models in clinical research, environmental microbiology, medical decision making, cognitive psychology, and epidemic transmission of social activities. I’ve written several articles and chapters on simulation methods (MCMCs and bootstrapping). Perhaps my biggest strength is exploratory and graphical data analysis. I have compiled a collection of my graphs to use when I teach graphing workshops that I'll be happy to distribute by email upon request.

My programming abilities are as strong as most professional software developers. I’ve developed: (a) four clinical databases with a SQL Server backend and C# middle and client tiers, (b) massive parallel statistical simulations, (c) medical decision making training aids, and (d) at least a dozen systems that manipulate and merge multiple data sources to produce real-time graphics and reporting. For these types of projects, I use several agile and TDD approaches. Regarding Linux, I use OU’s supercomputer (which runs Red Hat) for spatial analyses.

My preferred statistical tool is R, and I have used it extensively since 2005 (I have also taught courses and workshops with SAS and SPSS). I’ve developed one R package (NlsyLinks) and have taught many programming and graphing workshops in R. The appeal of literate programming and reproducible research prompted me to manage my R projects in GitHub and R-Forge and to use knitr/Sweave/ LaTeX for reports. R’s reporting ecosystem has allowed me to communicate better with a new audience (specifically, policy makers and politicians in the past two years), who have relatively limited statistical training.

A list of my publications can be found at Google Scholar. Additional information is available on LinkedIn and ResearchGate.
